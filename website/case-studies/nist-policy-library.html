NIST Security Policy Library

Three written policy documents defining how passwords, system changes, and new employee accounts get handled — the same rules help desk technicians enforce every day, based on NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) security guidelines.

NIST 800-53 NIST 800-63 Policy writing Change control

Why no screenshots on this page? This project's deliverable is the written policy itself, not a hands-on lab — there's no console or terminal to screenshot. Instead, each card below links to the actual document I wrote, so you can open the real thing instead of just taking my word for it. (These were written for "SnowBe Online," a fictional company used as the practice scenario in this Full Sail assignment — not a real employer.)

Password Standard

Passwords must be at least 12 characters long, mix upper/lower-case letters, numbers, and symbols, and can't be based on the person's name or birthdate. Passwords are changed every 90 days, are never shared with anyone — not even a supervisor — and are never written down; a password manager is used to store them instead.

Change Control

Before any change goes live on a real system: someone requests it, someone else reviews it, a manager approves it, it gets implemented, then it gets verified afterward to confirm it actually worked — and every step gets written down. No change goes live without a plan to undo it if something breaks.

New Account Procedure

Every new account starts with a manager's request and IT approval before it's created. IT then sets up the account with only the access needed for that person's job and turns on multi-factor authentication (MFA). The new hire gets notified, changes their temporary password on first login, and the manager double-checks their access is correct — with every step logged for an audit trail.

Help desk relevance

Most entry-level candidates never write policy — they just click through tickets. Writing these documents means I understand why account-creation and change-management tickets exist in the first place, not just the steps to close them.
