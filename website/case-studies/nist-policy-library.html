NIST Security Policy Library

Three written policy documents defining how passwords, system changes, and new employee accounts get handled — the same rules help desk technicians enforce every day, based on NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) security guidelines.

NIST 800-53 NIST 800-63 Policy writing Change control

Why no screenshots on this page? This project's deliverable is the written policy itself, not a hands-on lab — there's no console or terminal to screenshot. The quick-reference cards below summarize what each document actually requires in plain terms.

Password Standard

Passwords must be at least 12 characters long, and every account needs a second login step (multi-factor authentication) beyond just the password. Passwords aren't force-changed on a schedule — instead they're checked against lists of passwords already leaked in past breaches, and only changed when there's an actual reason to suspect one is compromised.

Change Control

Before any change goes live on a real system: someone requests it, someone else reviews it, a manager approves it, it gets implemented, then it gets verified afterward to confirm it actually worked — and every step gets written down. No change goes live without a plan to undo it if something breaks.

New Account Procedure

Every new employee account needs a request and a manager's approval before it's created. Each account only gets the minimum access needed for that person's job ("least privilege") and is unique to one person — never shared. The moment someone leaves the company, their account is disabled that same day.

Help desk relevance

Most entry-level candidates never write policy — they just click through tickets. Writing these documents means I understand why account-creation and change-management tickets exist in the first place, not just the steps to close them.
